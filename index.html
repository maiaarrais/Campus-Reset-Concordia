Our story

Built for the JMSB woman who's doing too much.

We exist because burnout, imposter syndrome, and competitive pressure are real — and because you deserve a space where none of that follows you in.

What we are

A club, a community, a breath of fresh air.

The Campus Reset is a student-run wellness and leadership club at the John Molson School of Business. We combine gentle movement, mental wellness practices, and genuine human connection — all designed around the reality of being a woman in business school.

We're not a fitness club. We're not a networking event. We're the space in between — where you can slow down, move your body, meet people who get it, and leave feeling like yourself again.

Our programming is free, inclusive, and built around JMSB's academic calendar so we show up exactly when you need us most: before midterms, during finals week, and every Tuesday morning when the week needs resetting.

What makes us different

Low-pressure, high-intention.

No competitive energy. No performance pressure. No need to already be "into wellness." You can show up having skipped breakfast, wearing your lecture outfit, with zero idea what you're doing — and you'll still belong here.

🌿 Accessible Movement

All bodies, all fitness levels, all schedules. We meet you where you are — not where you think you should be.

💛 Mental Wellness First

We normalize talking about academic stress, burnout, and imposter syndrome — and then we do something about it together.

🤝 Real Community

Collabs, partnerships with other clubs, and events that feel like something a friend planned — not a committee.

🎓 Built for JMSB

We know the case competitions, the internship pressure, the 8am finance class. Our schedule and programming reflect that.

Who is this for?

You're a woman at JMSB. You're driven, capable, and probably overloaded right now. You might be dealing with academic stress, imposter syndrome, burnout, or just the quiet loneliness of not yet finding your people. You want to take care of yourself but don't know where to start. You're exactly who we built this for.

Movement · 3 min read

5-Minute Movement Reset Between Classes

By The Campus Reset Team · Movement & Wellness · Concordia JMSB

You've been sitting for 90 minutes, your back is stiff, and you have exactly 10 minutes before your next lecture. You won't make it to the gym. You don't have time to change. But you have 5 minutes — and that's enough.

Why movement between classes matters

Research on sedentary behavior in university students shows that brief movement breaks improve both cognitive performance and mood. You don't need a full workout to reset — you need circulation, breath, and a moment outside your head.

The 5-minute reset (no equipment, no space required)

  • Minute 1: Walk to a window or outside. Literally just walk and look at something far away. Your eyes need a break too.
  • Minute 2: 10 shoulder rolls back, 10 neck circles. Do this at your desk or in the hallway — no one will notice, and if they do, they'll wish they were doing it too.
  • Minute 3: 3 deep breaths — inhale for 4 counts, hold for 2, exhale for 6. Repeat. This is the one that actually works.
  • Minute 4: 10 calf raises (standing wherever you are), then shake out your hands and wrists.
  • Minute 5: Stand still, close your eyes for 30 seconds. Name 3 things you can hear. Come back to your body.

Make it a habit at JMSB

The MB building has outdoor terraces and quiet stairwells that are perfect for a quick reset. The Hall building atrium is another go-to. Build this into your transition time between classes — treat it like it's part of your schedule, because it is.

Want to do this with other people? Our Walk & Talk event is exactly this, on Tuesdays at 12:30. Come once, and you'll understand why it's our most popular event.

Ready to make movement a regular part of your week? Join The Campus Reset and get access to weekly events, resources, and a community that gets it.

Stress · 4 min read

Exam Week Stress Routine That Actually Works

By The Campus Reset Team · Stress Relief · Concordia JMSB

The week before finals is not the time for a dramatic wellness overhaul. It's the time for a simple, repeatable routine that keeps you from completely falling apart. This one takes 15 minutes a day and makes a real difference.

Morning (5 minutes)

Before you open your laptop or check your phone: drink a glass of water, do 5 minutes of light stretching (reach for the ceiling, fold forward, roll up slowly), and set one intention for the day. Not a to-do list — one intention. "Today I will be kind to myself while I study." That's enough.

Midday (5 minutes)

Go outside. Even for 5 minutes. No headphones, no scrolling. Just walk around the block or sit on the steps of the Hall building. Your nervous system needs a break from fluorescent lighting and ambient laptop noise.

Evening (5 minutes)

Before bed: write down 3 things you did today (not 3 things you should have done). Breathe. Put your phone across the room. Sleep is studying — your brain consolidates information while you rest. Sacrificing sleep for another hour of notes is almost always a net loss.

The exam week thing nobody tells you

Imposter syndrome spikes during exam season. Everyone around you looks like they have it together. They don't. They're doing the same thing you are. Come to our Exam De-Stress event — being in a room with other people who are managing (not perfect, just managing) is its own kind of medicine.

Mobility · 3 min read

Beginner-Friendly Mobility You Can Do On Campus

By The Campus Reset Team · Mobility · Concordia JMSB

You don't need a yoga mat, a gym membership, or any prior experience to take care of your body on campus. These 4 moves can be done in any quiet corner of the MB building, the library, or even a stairwell between classes.

Move 1: Standing hip circles

Stand with feet hip-width apart. Put your hands on your hips. Make slow, large circles with your hips — 5 each direction. This undoes hours of sitting and feels immediately good. Do this anywhere.

Move 2: Doorway chest opener

Stand in any doorway. Place your forearms on the door frame at shoulder height. Gently lean forward until you feel a stretch across your chest. Hold for 30 seconds. This counteracts the forward-head posture we all develop from staring at screens.

Move 3: Seated figure-four stretch

Sitting in a chair, cross one ankle over the opposite knee. Gently press down on the bent knee and hinge forward slightly. Hold for 30 seconds each side. Your hips will thank you.

Move 4: Wall breathing

Stand with your back against a wall, feet 6 inches from the baseboard. Feel the back of your skull, shoulders, and lower back touch the wall. Breathe deeply for 1 minute. This resets your posture and your nervous system simultaneously.

Want to practice together?

Our Stretch Reset event is built around exactly these kinds of moves — accessible, practical, and designed for students who spend most of their day at a desk. No experience needed, ever.

Mindset · 5 min read

Dealing with Imposter Syndrome in Business School

By The Campus Reset Team · Mindset · Concordia JMSB

Business school is designed to make you feel like you don't belong. The case competitions, the networking events, the people who seem to know everything already — it's a lot. And if you're a woman at JMSB, you're navigating all of that with an extra layer of invisible pressure.

First: it's not just you

Imposter syndrome is especially prevalent among high-achieving women in competitive academic environments. That doesn't make it easier to feel, but it does mean the thing you're experiencing is a documented, widely shared phenomenon — not a personal character flaw.

What actually helps (not just "believe in yourself")

  • Document your wins. Keep a running list of things you did well. Not for anyone else — for the moments when your brain tells you you've never done anything right.
  • Name the voice. When imposter syndrome speaks, give it a name. Externalizing it ("there goes Patricia again") creates distance between you and the thought.
  • Talk to people who are honest. Not people who tell you you're great — people who are also navigating the same pressure. Community is the antidote.
  • Move your body. It sounds too simple, but physical movement interrupts the anxiety spiral that imposter syndrome feeds on. A 20-minute walk changes your neurochemistry.

The JMSB-specific version

There's something particular about being a woman in a business school environment — the unspoken pressure to be both competent and likeable, the case rooms where certain voices get heard more than others, the internship season where everything feels like a competition. This club exists partly because we recognized that women at JMSB need a space where none of that is present.

You don't have to process this alone. Join our community — a space built specifically for women navigating exactly what you're navigating.

Routine · 4 min read

A Morning Routine for People Who Are Not Morning People

By The Campus Reset Team · Energy & Routine · Concordia JMSB

Every morning routine article assumes you wake up feeling inspired and have 90 minutes before you need to leave. Here's one for the rest of us — the people with 8am Finance, a 25-minute commute, and a genuine dislike of mornings.

The only rule: don't pick up your phone first

Before anything else, give yourself 5 minutes before the world has access to you. This is the foundation. Everything else is optional.

The 10-minute version (minimum viable morning)

  • Drink water before coffee.
  • Open a window or step outside for 2 minutes of natural light.
  • Do 5 slow, deep breaths.
  • Ask yourself: what's one thing I'm doing today that matters?

The 20-minute version (when you have it)

Add 5 minutes of gentle movement (the mobility moves from our other post work great here), 5 minutes of journaling or just sitting quietly with your coffee, and one minute of setting an intention for the day.

The energy thing nobody talks about

Energy management is more important than time management for students. A 20-minute investment in your morning state pays off in 3–4 hours of better focus and lower anxiety during the day. The math is very much in favor of the morning routine.
